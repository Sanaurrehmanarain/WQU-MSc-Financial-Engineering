Modeling Non-Stationarity, Cointegration, and Estimation Using VECM¶

1. Demonstration¶

I used monthly Brent and WTI crude oil prices and applied log transformation to stabilize variance. Then, I checked for non-stationarity using the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test. Both series were found to be non-stationary in levels.

To test for a long-run equilibrium relationship between the two series, I applied the Johansen cointegration test. The result indicated the presence of at least one cointegrating vector.

I then estimated a Vector Error Correction Model (VECM), which captures both short-term dynamics and long-run equilibrium adjustment.

2. Interpretation of Results¶

ADF Test Results¶

  • Brent ADF p-value: 0.162
  • WTI ADF p-value: 0.0697

Both p-values > 0.05 → Non-stationary in levels.

Johansen Cointegration Test¶

The test detects 1 cointegrating relationship → the two series are cointegrated.

VECM Model Summary¶

  • Cointegrating equation: Brent ≈ 1.018 × WTI
  • Indicates strong long-term equilibrium.
  • Adjustment coefficients (ec1) are not statistically significant → short-term corrections are weak.

4. Insights¶

  1. Non-stationarity: Brent and WTI are non-stationary in levels, confirmed by ADF tests.
  2. Cointegration: Johansen test shows a long-term equilibrium between the two series.
  3. Equilibrium Relationship: Brent ≈ 1.018 × WTI.
  4. Short-Term Dynamics: Weak adjustment (insignificant ec1 coefficients), indicating slow return to equilibrium.
  5. Model Use: Suitable for long-term forecasting and relationship modeling, but not ideal for short-term prediction without additional enhancements.
